You may have noticed that it was sometimes a bit difficult to recall sentences word-for-word, even though you were recalling them almost immediately. This is totally normal!

In fact, one of the things we're investigating is how you might forget different parts of the sentence. For instance, some of the sentences looked like (1) below, where a subject noun is far away from the verb it's associated with. Look at the subject "key" in the sentences below.

(1) The key to the cabinets obviously are on the table

(2) * The key are on the table

Why does (1) sound so much better than (2)? Well in English, verbs change their form based on if their subject is singular or plural. We say "The keys are" but "The key is".

But interestingly enough, when there's a plural word between between a singular subject noun and its verb, our judgments about what the verb should look like can change!

We're looking to understand how you manipulate this wording in memory, once you've seen the whole sentence, had a second to process and maintain it, and then are made to reproduce it. Basically, how do the language processes in your own head affect the way you internalize a sentence?
